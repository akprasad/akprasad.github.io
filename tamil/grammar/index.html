Tamil grammar

Published 2025-03-21

Welcome! This page is a concise summary of modern Tamil grammar. It focuses on formal Tamil but also has notes on the informal Tamil commonly used in Chennai. For more details and explanations, see Jeyapandian Kottalam's Learning Tamil by yourself or your favorite textbook.

Basic points:
  • Fluency comes from exposure to real language, not from studying grammar. But, grammar can help us make sense of unusual things and look them up in a dictionary.

  • In Tamil, we express meaning by adding one or more suffixes to a word base. Generally, studying Tamil grammar means studying these suffixes and how to use them.

  • Formal Tamil is based on 13th-century usage and is standard across the Tamil world. Informal Tamil is used at home and in conversation, and it can vary widely from one community to the next. Almost all written Tamil is formal, and almost all spoken Tamil is informal.

  • Even if you care only about informal Tamil, formal Tamil is still very helpful because most textbooks, dictionaries, and other learning resources focus on it. With time and exposure, you will surely gain an intuition for how to convert between formal and informal styles.

Nouns

Nouns follow this pattern:

(base) + (plurality) + (case)

Base words are things like பையன் (boy), வீடு (house), மரம் (tree), etc. By default, base words express the singular and act as the subject of a sentence, as in பையன் சாப்பிட்டான் ("The boy ate."). To express the plural or change the role of the base word in a sentence, we use extra suffixes.

Base changes

If a case suffix is used, some bases might change:

Base endingWith case suffix
-டு-ட்டு
-ம்-த்து

Thus வீடு → வீட்டுக்கு, மரம் → மரத்துக்கு.

Plurality

By default, nouns are singular. We add a suffix to make them plural:

FormalInformalMeaning
-கள்-nga(ɭ)(plural)

-nga(ɭ) means that we use -nga by default and -ngaɭ if another suffix follows, as in paiyangaɭukku ("for the boys").

Verbs

Components

All verbs are built upon these three components:

  • Root — the base form of the verb, as listed in dictionaries.

  • Infinitive — created by adding -அ to the root. Many roots use -க instead.

  • Adverb — created by adding a past-tense suffix to the root.

Consonant doubling

In formal Tamil, the consonants க், c, த், and ப் double in these circumstances:

  • When they follow the -ஐ suffix.
  • When they follow the -கு suffix.
  • When they follow the infinitive.
  • Always when they follow adverbs in the following verb classes: வாங்கு, பார், சாப்பிடு
  • Never when they follow adverbs in the following verb classes: பிழி, இரு, உட்கார், விழி, கொள், கொல், or செய்.

Various other minor rules also apply.
