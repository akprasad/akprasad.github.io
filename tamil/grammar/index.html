Introduction

This page contains the essential grammar of spoken Tamil, specifically the informal language used in the popular media of Tamil Nadu. It also contains some notes on formal Tamil, which can be heard in formal speech and most songs.

This page gives you quick answers to quick questions. If you are a complete beginner, try skimming through it to get a sense of how Tamil works.

Before we begin, some basics:

  • In Tamil, we express meaning by adding one or more suffixes to a word base. Generally, studying Tamil grammar means studying these suffixes.

  • Formal Tamil is based on 13th-century usage and is standard across the Tamil world. Informal Tamil is used at home and in conversation, and it can vary widely from one community to the next. Almost all written Tamil is formal, and almost all spoken Tamil is informal.

  • For learners, grammar is at best a supplement to the real task of engaging with interesting Tamil content. We hope this page can give you quick answers so that you can return to more interesting things.

If you want to study rules in a structured program, see Jeyapandian Kottalam's Learning Tamil by Yourself or your favorite textbook. If you want an exhaustive explanation of Tamil's rules, see Thomas Lehmann's A Grammar of Modern Tamil or your favorite reference book.

Nouns

Nouns are naming words like "house" and "tree." They follow this pattern:

(base) + (plurality) + (case)

Notes:

  • Base words are things like paiyan (boy), vīɖu (house), maram (tree), etc.

  • By default, base words express the singular and act as the subject of a sentence, as in paiyan sāppʈʈān ("The boy ate."). To express the plural or change the role of the base word in a sentence, we use extra suffixes.

Plurality

By default, nouns are singular. We add a suffix to make them plural:

FormalInformalMeaning
-kaɭ-ṅga(ɭ)(plural)

-ṅga(ɭ) means that we use -ṅga by default and -ṅgaɭ if another suffix follows:

  • maram
    tree

  • maraṅga
    trees

  • maraṅgaɭukku
    for the trees

Base changes

A noun's base form may change in certain contexts, such as:

  • When a case suffix is added directly to the base.

  • When the noun is compounded with another noun.

Common changes:

-ɖu-ʈʈu
-m-ttu

Examples:

  • maram → marattukku
    tree → to the tree

  • vīɖu → vīʈʈukku
    house → to the house

Verbs

Components

All verbs are built upon these three components:

  • Root — the base form of the verb, as listed in dictionaries.

  • Infinitive — created by adding -a to the root. Many roots use -kka instead.

  • Adverb — created by adding a past-tense suffix to the root.

Verbal adjectives

FormalInformalMeaning
(root) + (tense) + -a—… who did / does / will do X1
(infinitive) + -āda—… who did not / does not / will not do X
  1. An exception is the future adjective, which uses the same form as the future neuter singular (varum, irukkum, etc.).

Examples:

  • nēttu vanda bas nāɭekki pōhum
    The bus that came yesterday will go tomorrow.

  • nāɭekki pōgum bas nēttu vandadu
    The bus that will go tomorrow came yesterday.

Consonant doubling

(formal Tamil only)

In formal Tamil, the consonants k, c, t, and p double in these circumstances:

  • When they follow the -ai suffix.

  • When they follow the -ku suffix.

  • When they follow the infinitive.

  • Always when they follow adverbs in the following verb classes: vāṅgu, pār, sāppiʈu

  • Never when they follow adverbs in the following verb classes: piʐi, iru, uʈkār, viʐi, koɭ, kol, or sey.

Various other minor rules also apply.
